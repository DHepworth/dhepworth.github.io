May 21st, 2022: Stuck in Oklahoma

I think it started with the architecture. I noticed that many new structures being built in the country lacked good design and thought for the community in which they were placed. They lacked friendly entranceways or pleasant views from the sides, or were so stripped of design that they felt inhuman and otherworldly. I started searching for answers online and discovered the non-profit Strong Towns, which gave me many of the words that I needed to describe my discomfort. For a time I felt that the frustration I felt as I walked around a neighborhood was due primarily to the choice and placement of its buildings, but as I grew to understand the complexity of the way in which a city was fashioned I began to understand why knowing of the problem would not necessarily provide any options for improvement.

From there I learned of city planning issues that sidetracked progress in most cities across the country, which helped me see how my dissatisfaction with modern building styles was linked to a greater systemic problem with the way things are built today. Other countries had embraced and benefitted from better modes of transportation, weaning themselves a bit from the dependence on personal motor vehicles, while the United States leaned ever deeper into car dependence. The feeling of disconnectedness became better defined as I continued my introspection, where I came to see that it goes beyond the destruction of community or collective thinking and into more personal issues like politics and beliefs. I can see more clearly now how many of these elements of our lives are interconnected and function in a tension of both communion and contradiction.
